### Noticing (Interpersonal Gap Awareness)

#### About This Practice

> "Always remember that it is impossible to speak in such a way that you cannot be misunderstood: there will always be some who misunderstand you." — Karl Popper

John L. Wallen, a social psychologist and pioneer in communication, identified something both humbling and liberating: there is always a gap between what we intend to convey and what another person actually receives. He called this the Interpersonal Gap.

We've all stumbled into this gap. A well-intended comment lands wrong. A gesture of care is received as criticism. We walk away confused, the other person walks away hurt, and neither of us fully understands what happened.

Imagine you've baked a beautiful birthday cake for a friend — chocolate, painstaking, made with love. When it comes time to serve it, your friend doesn't take a bite. You leave feeling unappreciated. You find out later your friend has a severe chocolate allergy, quietly managed, and assumed you knew. They felt the cake choice was insensitive. Left unaddressed, a gap that was nobody's fault has the potential to end a friendship.

The Interpersonal Gap is insidious by nature — buzzing quietly in the background of nearly every interaction, from the checkout line to our most intimate relationships. Becoming aware of it is the first step to bridging it.

When we send a message, we bundle our words with personal beliefs, experiences, tone, and body language — and assume our intentions will arrive intact. But the message is received and filtered through someone else's entirely different set of lived experiences and cultural lenses. The result is often not what we intended.

Interpersonal Gap Awareness is the practice of recognizing that this gap exists, and slowing down enough to check our assumptions, communicate with greater precision, and remain curious when something doesn't land the way we hoped.

At Intentional Society, we use Interpersonal Gap Awareness as an important foundational understanding. Sometimes, simply knowing the gap exists is enough to give us the pause we need — allowing us to communicate with clearer intention, more sensitivity, and less reactivity when things go sideways.

When they do go sideways, this awareness gives us a place to look. Not to assign blame, but to find shared humanity — and perhaps an opening to close the gap through curiosity and honest conversation.

#### The Practice

Session length: 60-70 minutes

#### Facilitator Overview

The practice builds up progressively through three layers of noticing, using several very short breakouts to isolate and focus on elements. The facilitator introduces each layer to the full group, sends pairs to breakout rooms for a short exercise, then does minimal orienting before moving to the next round.

- Keep the main-room debriefs brief — resist the urge to over-process between layers
- We run this with the same partners for all the rooms, in order to reduce the number of variables changing across the rounds

#### Opening — Framing the Gap (5-10 min)

Before any breakouts, introduce the Interpersonal Gap concept to the whole group. Draw from the text above, your own understanding, and we have [some diagrams here](https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1RLacJsJ53IZj1DfukbMJcTwGDgMb7ODvmWSAfSV2EG0/edit?usp=sharing) that may be helpful.

#### Warmup — Projection Improv (8 min)

Introduce: First, we're going to deliberately project as wildly as possible — knowing it's all made up. We're playing with exaggerating a natural tendency in order to contrast with our later steps.

- Breakout: pairs, 3 minutes
- Instruction: 'You [perception]... therefore you...' Claim everything you could possibly conclude from any tiny piece of evidence. Go wild.
- Return: debrief, probably with laughter. Ask: What did you notice? Even when a projection is mostly right, how does it feel?

Chat instructions:

> 🎭 Warmup — Projection Improv. Deliberately make wild assumptions! 'You [perception]... therefore you...'<br>
> Claim everything you could possibly conclude from every tiny bit of evidence.

#### Layer 1 — Physical Noticing (8 min)

Introduce to full group: We begin with noticing statements that attempt to be unarguably true: the raw data of the senses. Nothing interpreted, nothing inferred.

- Breakout setup: pairs, 3 minutes
- Instruction: Take turns. Use 'I notice...' with what you're seeing, hearing, touching — only what your senses directly receive.
- Return: <5min debrief in main room. Ask: What came up? Was it harder than expected?

Chat instructions:

> 🧭 Layer 1 — Physical Noticing<br>
> Taking turns, "I notice… [physical/sensory]"<br>
> Noticing physical observations and sensations, making your statements unarguable

#### Layer 2 — Feeling Noticing (8 min)

Introduce: We're going to add another layer that's already arriving in your brain alongside physical sensation: your feelings and emotions. (We're not distinguishing between the two here.) (You can introduce [feelingswheel.com](https://feelingswheel.com/) if it seems useful to clarify what words count as feelings.)

- Breakout setup: pairs, 3 minutes (same or new partners — your call)
- Instruction: Name both a sensation and a feeling. They don't have to be linked.
- Return: <5min debrief. Ask: How do we respond to hearing feelings?

Chat instructions:

> 💛 Layer 2 — Emotional Noticing<br>
> "I notice… [physical/sensory] and I notice I'm feeling… [feeling]"<br>
> Noticing what is arising in our bodies, welcoming and observing our feelings

#### Layer 3 — Story Noticing (9 min)

Introduce: Now the stories. After 30 seconds of silent eye contact on camera, each person shares what narratives arose — held lightly, clearly owned as theirs.

- Breakout setup: pairs, 4 minutes
- Instruction: 30 seconds of silent eye contact, then take turns. This time, notice a sensation or feeling plus a story with "and my story about that is…". Don't answer the stories or confirm/deny them (though you might have a story arise in yourself in response!)
- Return: <5min debrief. Ask: What did you notice this time — what was different?

Chat instructions:

> 💭 Layer 3 — Story Noticing<br>
> 30 seconds silent eye contact, then:<br>
> "(Hearing that,) I notice… [physical/sensory] (or) I notice I'm feeling… [feeling] and my story about that is… [interpretation]"

#### Putting It Together — Story Checking (11 min)

Introduce: Now we bring it all together. Notice, name your story, then check it by asking about the other person's experience. When you are asked, give yourself a moment right there to recheck what is arising for you.

- Breakout setup: pairs, 6 minutes
- Instruction: I notice... plus my story about that is... what's that like to hear? You can respond directly, briefly, or flow straight into another noticing.
- Return: <5min debrief.

Chat instructions:

> 🔄 Putting it together — Story Checking<br>
> "I notice…" plus "My story about that is…"<br>
> …and end it with "What's that like to hear?"<br>
> Brief response, then take your own turn to notice + story + check

#### Closing (10-15 min)

After the story-checking debrief, zoom out for a final debrief of the whole sequence. Name the arc: physical → feeling → story → checking.

Ask: What are you noticing now about all our noticing practice?

These are the layers we normally compress into a single instant (or skip). The practice is simply slowing them down enough to see each one separately.
